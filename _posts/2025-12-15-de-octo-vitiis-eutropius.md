## Historical Context: Visigothic Spain After Conversion

The *De Octo Vitiis* was composed around 600 AD in Visigothic Spain, during a period of remarkable cultural and ecclesiastical vitality. Just eleven years earlier, in 589, King Reccared I had formally converted the Visigothic kingdom from Arianism to Nicene Christianity at the Third Council of Toledo—an event that transformed the religious landscape of the Iberian Peninsula and created new opportunities for monastic and theological development.

Eutropius wrote this treatise as a letter to Bishop Peter of Ercavica (modern Cañaveruelas in Cuenca province), addressing the spiritual formation of monks at a time when Spanish monasticism was flourishing. The Servitanum monastery, where Eutropius served as abbot before his elevation to the episcopate, was a center of learning and ascetic practice that drew on both Eastern and Western monastic traditions.

## The Transmission of Desert Spirituality

The text reflects the transmission of Egyptian desert spirituality to the Latin West. The eight-vice schema Eutropius presents derives ultimately from **Evagrius Ponticus** (345–399), whose list of eight *logismoi* (evil thoughts) was transmitted to the West primarily through **John Cassian's** *Collationes* (Conferences) and *De institutis coenobiorum* (Institutes). Eutropius's concise treatment shows how this Eastern framework had become thoroughly naturalized in Western monasticism by the sixth century.

The historical significance of this period cannot be overstated: within a generation, **Gregory the Great** would condense the eight vices into seven deadly sins in his *Moralia in Job*, the formulation that would dominate medieval spirituality. Eutropius's text thus represents the **final flowering of the older eight-vice tradition** before its transformation.

## The Eight Principal Vices

Eutropius presents the eight vices in order:

1. **Gastrimargia** (γαστριμαργία) — Gluttony, literally "madness of the belly"
2. **Fornicatio** — Sexual sin
3. **Philargyria** (φιλαργυρία) — Avarice, love of money
4. **Ira** — Anger
5. **Tristitia** — Sadness, dejection
6. **Acedia** (ἀκηδία) — Spiritual torpor, listlessness
7. **Cenodoxia** (κενοδοξία) — Vainglory, empty glory
8. **Superbia** — Pride

## Key Theological Insights

### The Causal Chain

Eutropius insists that the first six vices are interconnected in a chain of causation:

> "de abundantia gastrimargiae fornicationem, de fornicatione philargyriam, de philargyria iram, de ira tristitiam, de tristitia acediam, necesse est pullulare"
>
> (from the abundance of gluttony, fornication necessarily sprouts; from fornication, avarice; from avarice, anger; from anger, sadness; from sadness, acedia)

This insight has practical implications: one must fight the vices in **reverse order**, cutting off each at its root by conquering the vice that feeds it.

### The Twin Peaks

The final two vices—vainglory and pride—operate differently. They attack not the struggling sinner but the victorious one:

> "in his vero duobus etiam victores post triumphos periclitamur"
>
> (in these two, even victors are endangered after their triumphs)

This psychological insight anticipates later spiritual writers' emphasis on spiritual pride as the subtlest and most dangerous sin.

### The Four Conjugations

Eutropius organizes the eight vices into four pairs:

- **Carnal**: Gluttony and Fornication
- **Psychic**: Avarice and Anger
- **Spiritual Lassitude**: Sadness and Acedia
- **Spiritual Presumption**: Vainglory and Pride

This pairing reflects different aspects of the soul's vulnerability and different strategies required for combat.

### Natural Origin of Passions

In a sophisticated theological move, Eutropius argues that the underlying impulses (*motus*) that give rise to the vices were originally implanted by God for good purposes and were corrupted only after Adam's fall:

> "voluntas Domini possessionem cordis nostri non vitiis, sed virtutibus naturaliter deputavit"
>
> (the Lord's will naturally assigned the possession of our heart not to vices but to virtues)

The appetites themselves are not evil; their **disordered expression** is. This theological anthropology aligns with the mainstream patristic tradition against Manichaean dualism.

## The Author: Saint Eutropius of Valencia

**Saint Eutropius of Valencia** (Latin: *Eutropius Valentinus*; fl. late 6th–early 7th century; d. c. 610) was a Spanish churchman who served successively as monk, abbot, and bishop during the critical period following the Visigothic conversion to Catholicism.

### Early Life and Monasticism

Eutropius entered the monastery of Servitanum (location uncertain, possibly near Valencia or in the Aragonese region), where he eventually rose to become abbot (*pater monachorum*). Isidore of Seville records that while still at Servitanum, Eutropius composed his letters on the vices and monastic discipline, indicating that his reputation as a spiritual writer preceded his episcopal career.

### Episcopal Ministry

Eutropius was elevated to the bishopric of Valencia (Latin: *Valentia*), one of the most important sees in Visigothic Spain. His tenure coincided with the consolidation of Catholic Christianity in the Visigothic kingdom under kings Reccared I, Liuva II, and Witteric.

### Legacy

Isidore of Seville's characterization of Eutropius's letter on the vices as *"valde utilem epistolam"* (a very useful letter) indicates that the text enjoyed authority in seventh-century Spain. The subsequent obscurity of Eutropius reflects not the quality of his work but the catastrophic disruption of Spanish church life by the Muslim invasion of 711.

Eutropius is venerated as a saint in the Spanish church. His feast day is traditionally celebrated on **May 23**.

## Scholarly Significance

The *De Octo Vitiis* survives in relatively few manuscripts, reflecting Eutropius's obscurity compared to more famous patristic authors. The text was preserved primarily through its inclusion in patristic collections and florilegia.

**Principal Editions:**
- The standard text appears in Migne's *Patrologia Latina*, volume 80, columns 9D–14D
- The editio princeps of Eutropius's letters was published by Cardinal Lorenzana in his collection of Spanish patristic texts
- Modern critical apparatus is lacking; no critical edition meeting contemporary scholarly standards has been published

**Related Texts:**
- Eutropius wrote a second letter to Peter of Ercavica, *De Districtione Monachorum* (On Monastic Discipline), preserved in the same manuscript tradition
- Isidore of Seville mentions both letters in his *De viris illustribus*, chapter 32, providing our earliest external testimony
