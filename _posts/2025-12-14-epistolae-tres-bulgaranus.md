## Historical Context: The Merovingian Crisis

These letters were written during one of the most dramatic periods in Merovingian history. The central figures mentioned include:

**Queen Brunhilda** (c. 543-613): The Visigothic princess who became queen of Austrasia and later regent of Burgundy. By 610 CE, she was in her late sixties, ruling through her great-grandsons Theuderic II (king of Burgundy) and Sigebert II (nominally king of Austrasia). Her ruthless consolidation of power had earned her many enemies.

**King Theuderic II** (587-613): Brunhilda's grandson and king of Burgundy. The letters accuse him and Brunhilda of inciting barbarian attacks against his cousin Theudebert II of Austrasia.

**King Theudebert II** (586-612): King of Austrasia, engaged in a bitter rivalry with Theuderic II. He would eventually be defeated and killed by Theuderic in 612.

**King Gundemar** (d. 612): Visigothic king of Spain, whom Bulgaranus serves. The letters concern diplomatic negotiations between the Goths and the Franks.

The Avars mentioned in Letter 2 were a nomadic people from the Eurasian steppes who had established a powerful khaganate in the Pannonian Basin. Their raids into Frankish territory were a constant threat during this period.

## The Author: Bulgaranus Comes

Bulgaranus Comes remains an enigmatic figure. "Comes" indicates the rank of count (Latin *comes*), suggesting he was a nobleman in the service of the Visigothic King Gundemar. His name may derive from a place-name or ethnic designation, though its exact origin is uncertain.

The letters reveal him as a skilled diplomatic correspondent, comfortable addressing a bishop as both a spiritual authority and a political player. His Latin is sophisticated, employing the formal diplomatic conventions expected of 7th-century correspondence while occasionally breaking into pointed accusations of bad faith.

## Structure and Content

### Letter 1: Request for United Prayer Against Barbarian Invasion

The first letter responds to alarming news: Queen Brunhilda and King Theuderic have allegedly incited "the most savage king of the barbarians" to attack King Theudebert. Bulgaranus requests:

1. Confirmation of the situation
2. Coordinated prayers and litanies across the provinces
3. Updates on diplomatic communications with Theudebert and Gundemar

The letter demonstrates how episcopal networks functioned as intelligence-gathering systems in the Merovingian world. Bishops were expected to share political information and coordinate religious responses to military crises.

### Letter 2: Peace Negotiations with the Goths and the Avar Threat

The longest of the three letters, this epistle concerns ongoing peace negotiations between King Theudebert and the Goths. Key points include:

- **Diplomatic Protocol**: Ambassadors have been exchanged, and money has been promised to cement the alliance
- **The Avar Threat**: Brunhilda and Theuderic have allegedly summoned the Avars to attack Theudebert's territory
- **Religious Outrage**: Bulgaranus expresses horror that Christians would "provoke savages to rage against Christians"
- **Call for Fasting**: He requests solemn fasts throughout the province to invoke divine protection

The letter provides valuable evidence for Gothic-Frankish diplomatic relations and the role of ecclesiastical figures as intermediaries.

### Letter 3: Reproach for Diplomatic Deception and Detained Ambassadors

The final letter marks a sharp change in tone. Bulgaranus accuses the bishop of duplicity:

- **Detained Ambassadors**: Gothic envoys named Totila and Gundirmir have been imprisoned at a place called Irapinas
- **Broken Treaties**: The peace "once bound together by the former leaders of both peoples now remains dissolved"
- **Property Disputes**: Queen Brunhilda's estates in Gothic territory (at Jubinianus and Cornelianus) are now in dispute
- **Divine Judgment**: Bulgaranus calls on "the most high prince" to "strike down with divine vengeance" the guilty party

This letter reveals the fragility of early medieval diplomacy and the personal stakes involved for the negotiators.

## Theological and Political Themes

### The Unity of Christendom

Bulgaranus repeatedly invokes shared Christian faith as the basis for peace:

> "However much we may be separated by temporal dominion of empire, it is fitting that we not be divided from the precepts of the eternal prince."

This rhetoric reflects the tension between the political fragmentation of post-Roman Europe and the ideal of Christian unity.

### Episcopal Responsibility

The letters assume that bishops bear direct responsibility for maintaining peace:

> "How can you take care, if in such times you are a bishop dedicated to God by princes to act for the unharmed charity of both sides, that the peace once bound together by the former leaders of both peoples now remains dissolved?"

### Divine Justice

Bulgaranus frequently invokes divine judgment on the guilty party, a common diplomatic convention that carried genuine theological weight:

> "Let the most high prince see this cause from his heavenly throne, and let him strike down with divine vengeance the party who is seen to have unjustly brought trouble upon the other party."

## Historical Significance

These letters provide rare first-hand evidence of:

1. **Gothic-Frankish Relations**: The complex diplomatic negotiations between Visigothic Spain and Merovingian Gaul
2. **Queen Brunhilda's Reputation**: Contemporary accusations of her ruthless political methods
3. **The Avar Threat**: How Merovingian kingdoms used (or were accused of using) barbarian allies against each other
4. **Episcopal Diplomacy**: The central role of bishops in early medieval politics
5. **Diplomatic Conventions**: The formal rhetoric and expectations of 7th-century correspondence

The letters' preservation in the *Patrologia Latina* suggests they were valued as examples of diplomatic correspondence, though they received limited scholarly attention until now.

## Noteworthy Excerpts

### On Christian Unity Across Political Divisions

> "Although the entire boundary of lands under the heavenly axis is seen to be established by the Lord as creator, and the division of all who flourish in power is confirmed by his marvelous ordering, it is nevertheless necessary that we, keeping his gospel voice, should both seek out and love those whom one faith in Christ unites."

### On Using Pagans Against Christians

> "It is unspeakable to declare to Catholic ears that persons under Christian religion, truly established far from the Lord, would provoke savages to rage against Christians."

### On Diplomatic Bad Faith

> "Who does not see through the character of your holiness, that you try to soothe with flattering snares of eloquence those things which you have made bitter by illicit actions?"

### On Episcopal Responsibility for Peace

> "You, most blessed man, whom we remember as being until now of benign spirit and vigilant in secular affairs and greatly fearing the Almighty—how can you take care... that the peace once bound together by the former leaders of both peoples now remains dissolved, when by your counsel it ought to have been strengthened to exist in perpetuity?"
