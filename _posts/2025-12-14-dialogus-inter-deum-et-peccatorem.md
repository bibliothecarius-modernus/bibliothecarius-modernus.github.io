## The Author: Pope Innocent III

Lotario dei Conti di Segni (c. 1160-1216) reigned as Pope Innocent III from 1198 to 1216, presiding over the medieval papacy at its zenith of political and spiritual influence. He approved the Franciscan Order after a prophetic dream, launched the Fourth Crusade (which infamously sacked Constantinople), and convened the Fourth Lateran Council in 1215—one of the most important councils in medieval church history.

Among the council's seventy decrees was Canon 21, *Omnis utriusque sexus*, which mandated that every Christian confess their sins to a priest at least once a year. This requirement transformed medieval religious life and established the sacrament of penance as a cornerstone of Catholic practice.

Before becoming pope, Innocent was a trained theologian at Paris and Bologna, and he wrote several theological and devotional works, including the famous *De contemptu mundi* (On the Contempt of the World). The *Dialogus inter Deum et Peccatorem* reveals the pastoral heart behind his later ecclesiastical reforms—a pope who understood the psychology of sin and repentance from the inside.

## The Manuscript Discovery

This dialogue was unknown to scholarship until the nineteenth century. Angelo Mai (1782-1854), the celebrated Vatican librarian and cardinal, discovered it in Vatican Palatine Codex 365 and published it in volume six of his *Spicilegium Romanum* (1841). As Mai notes in his editorial preface, the manuscript bears the inscription "Dialogue of Innocent the Third Between God and a Sinner."

Whether Innocent composed it before or after becoming pope remains uncertain. As Mai observes, "Innocent does not speak about himself in it, but rather presents the character of any sinner whatsoever speaking with God." The work's absence from the *Gesta Innocentii III* (the near-contemporary biography of Innocent) is not surprising, Mai explains, since many of Innocent's writings "both published and unpublished, were unknown to the author of the Gesta."

Only five medieval manuscripts of this text are known to survive.

## Structure and Argument

The dialogue takes the form of a penitential exchange in which a sinner attempts to pray to God using various biblical formulas, only to have God reject each attempt and expose the sinner's hypocrisy.

### The Three Failed Prayers

**First Attempt: "Our Father"**
The sinner tries to use the Lord's Prayer, but God objects: "Because you do not present yourself to me as a son, you cannot worthily invoke me as father in that prayer." To call God "Father" requires acting like a son—that is, keeping his commandments.

**Second Attempt: "Lord, have mercy"**
The sinner switches to David's formula from the Psalms, but God responds with a striking psychological test: if you were alone with a woman you desired, and someone you feared burst in, would you not immediately stop? Yet for God's sake you do not refrain, "though you know that it displeases me." This proves the sinner fears humans more than God.

**Third Attempt: "God, be merciful to me, a sinner"**
The sinner adopts the tax collector's prayer from Luke 18, but God exposes deeper idolatry: "That is your god which is more loved by you." If avarice rules, money is your god; if gluttony, your belly is your god. The sinner who loves any vice more than God cannot invoke God without mockery.

### The Marriage Metaphor

God develops a striking image of spiritual adultery: the soul is God's bride, but by loving sin, it has "brought in the devil like an external lover." Using Isaiah 28:20, God says "the bed is so narrow that one of the two must fall off"—either God or the devil must depart. Because the soul has chosen sin, God has withdrawn: "I cannot now hear the cry of your voice."

### The Path to Restoration

When the sinner finally asks how to restore God's presence, God prescribes the classic penitential formula:
1. **Contrition** ("Rend your hearts and not your garments")
2. **Confession** to a priest
3. **Satisfaction** according to priestly judgment

But the sinner raises a theological objection: how can he produce contrition when it is a gift of grace he cannot merit? God responds with sophisticated scholastic theology, distinguishing between *merit* (what one earns) and *congruity* (what makes one suitable to receive grace). The sinner cannot make himself good, but he can "apply his free will" to God through meditation on divine benefits. This disposition does not earn grace but makes its reception fitting.

The dialogue ends with the memorable image of opening one's eyes to receive sunlight: "Nothing is required for them to see except only what is within his power to do—namely, to open his eyes—since the brightness of the sun is prepared outside."

## Theological Significance

The *Dialogus* anticipates the sacramental theology that Innocent would later codify at the Fourth Lateran Council. Its emphasis on priestly confession (not merely contrition alone) reflects the developing medieval theology that would distinguish "attrition" (imperfect sorrow from fear of punishment) from "contrition" (perfect sorrow from love of God), and debate whether either was sufficient without sacramental absolution.

The dialogue also reflects the Augustinian tradition on grace and free will: human beings cannot merit initial grace, but they can dispose themselves to receive it. This "congruent merit" (*meritum de congruo*) became a standard distinction in scholastic theology.

Most striking is the psychological sophistication of God's arguments. Rather than abstract theological propositions, God uses vivid scenarios—the illicit encounter interrupted by a feared witness, the adulteress with a legitimate husband and an external lover—to expose the sinner's self-deception. This is pastoral theology aimed at breaking through rationalization.

## Noteworthy Excerpts from the Text (Translated)

### On True Fear of God

> "If you were in some secret place alone with a woman whom you greatly desired, prepared and greatly inflamed toward her to fulfill bodily lust, would you not, if someone whom you feared burst in, immediately abandon the deed begun and flee in fear of him? ... But for my sake you do not refrain from doing such things even before nearby eyes, though you know that it displeases me and that I forbid you to do it."

### On Functional Idolatry

> "That is your god which is more loved by you. For if you are greedy, you worship and love money as an idolater worships God. For avarice, as my apostle testifies, 'is the service of idols.' If you are a belly-worshiper or glutton, your belly is your god."

### On Grace and Free Will

> "I do not require of you that you make yourself good—something which I know you cannot do, since this is in me—but that by applying your free will to me and meditating on divine benefits, you make yourself suitable to me. If you do this—since this is within you—you will undoubtedly receive the infusion of my grace, which I am always ready to give from my goodness to anyone who effectively wills to receive it."

### The Eye and the Sun

> "So it is with one who has closed eyes. Nothing is required for them to see except only what is within his power to do—namely, to open his eyes—since the brightness of the sun is prepared outside, which will immediately illuminate the opened eyes for seeing. By this example, consider clearly that divine grace is prepared to infuse itself into a person; only the congruity of suitability in him is required, found through the application of free will."
